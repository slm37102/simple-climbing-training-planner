# Climbing Training Plans (Bouldering & Sport)

**Executive Summary:** Periodized training is essential for peak climbing performance. Bouldering (short, intense problems) and sport climbing (longer endurance routes) demand different emphases: boulderers train maximal strength/power, while sport climbers build aerobic and anaerobic endurance. Training typically follows progressive **phases** (e.g. Base/Conditioning → Strength → Power → Power-Endurance → Taper) organized into macrocycles (6–24+ weeks). Common periodization models include **linear** (sequential phases), **undulating** (frequent intensity shifts), **block** (focused blocks of specific quality), and **polarized** (mostly very low or high intensity work). For competitions or trips, one plans to *peak* at the event: the final phase is timed to match the goal, with a taper (reduced volume, maintained intensity) 1–3 weeks before to ensure freshness. For example, Gresham’s year-long plan uses sequential 6‑week blocks (Conditioning → Endurance → Strength → Power-Endurance → Strength/Power → Endurance → Power-Endurance → Peaking)【14†L25-L34】. By contrast, a concurrent or undulating approach (à la Adam Ondra) mixes intensity daily or weekly to balance both strength and endurance【51†L79-L83】.

## 1. Periodization & Phases  
- **Base (General Conditioning):**  Build an aerobic/aerobic base and general strength (e.g. low-intensity climbing, easy circuits, general fitness). This prevents early burnout【14†L42-L50】. Typical duration: 4–8 weeks.  
- **Strength:** Focus on maximal strength (limit bouldering, heavy hangboard hangs, weighted pull-ups)【26†L130-L139】【29†L171-L180】. Duration often 3–6 weeks.  
- **Power:** Develop explosive power (campus board, dynamic bouldering moves, plyometrics). Follows strength or runs concurrently.  
- **Power-Endurance:** Train repeated efforts (e.g. 4×4 boulder circuits, interval routes, repeaters on hangboard) to sustain climbs of 15–40 moves【58†L90-L94】. Usually 3–6 weeks.  
- **Peaking/Taper:** In final 1–2 weeks before competition/send, volume drops and intensity remains high to “sharpen” performance and recover. For example, Gresham’s plan inserts 3–4 day rest breaks between blocks and a final taper for top form【58†L90-L94】【58†L132-L140】.  
- **Differences (Boulder vs Sport):** Boulder training emphasizes maximal strength/power (e.g. 100m sprinter), sport emphasizes stamina (800m runner)【51†L79-L83】. In practice, a bouldering block might skip long endurance, whereas a lead-climbing block includes ARC and interval work. However, even boulderers benefit from some endurance to avoid plateaus【42†L198-L202】.  

**Periodization Models:**  
- *Linear (Classic):* Gradually shift focus (e.g. 3–4w Base → 3–4w Strength → 3–4w Power → Peak). Common in structured plans【14†L25-L34】.  
- *Block:* Long blocks dedicated to one quality (e.g. 6–8 weeks pure strength). Allows deep adaptation before moving on.  
- *Undulating (Non-linear):* Vary intensity/volume daily or weekly. For example, alternate heavy (strength) days with easy or power days within a week.  
- *Concurrent/Polarized:* Mix training emphases throughout (e.g. Ondra’s mix of boulder and endurance sessions); or use mostly low-intensity and some high-intensity (polarized), minimizing moderate work.  

Athletes should plan to peak at target events. For competitions or big sending trips, place the highest-intensity block so that it finishes about 1–3 weeks before the goal, with a taper period (e.g. cutting volume by 40–60%) preceding it. During taper, maintain intensity (e.g. keep doing limit boulders or hard moves) but reduce sets/reps and add rest.

Each phase’s objectives:
- **Base:** Increase volume and aerobic capacity (open climbs, ARC sessions) with moderate intensity. 
- **Strength:** Increase maximal strength and power (limit boulders, weighted hangs, campus, hangboard protocols【26†L130-L139】).
- **Power:** Express explosive power (dynamic boulders, campus drills, plyometrics).
- **Power-Endurance:** Improve ability to link moves (4×4 boulder circuits, interval routes, repeaters【29†L231-L239】).
- **Taper:** Sharpen performance; reduce volume (e.g. ≤50% sets) while keeping intensity high.

## 3. Microcycles & Weekly Templates

**Base-Phase Week (Examples):** Focus on volume and general fitness, minimal fatigue. For instance, Lattice Training suggests for boulder-base weeks: 
- 1× Anaerobic Capacity session (after rest; e.g. 4×4 boulder circuit)  
- 1× Strength/Power boulder session + supplementary S&C (fingerboard, weighted pull-ups, antagonist work)【44†L248-L257】  
- 1–2× Open climbing sessions (moderate routes for technique/mileage)  
- 1× Flexibility/mobility (e.g. lower-body stretching)  
- 1× Shoulder/rotator cuff work【44†L248-L257】  

For a sport-base week: 
- 1× Aerobic endurance session (e.g. long climbs at ~5–6a with light pump)【44†L275-L284】  
- 0.5× Strength/Power + 0.5× Aerobic (split session of weighted hangs and moderate climbing)  
- 1× Supplementary S&C (fingerboard, pull-ups, antagonist exercises)【44†L275-L284】  
- 1–2× Open climbing (pyramids or multiple-route day)  
- 1× Flexibility and 1× Shoulder stability (band rotations)【44†L275-L284】.  

**Strength/Power Week:** Emphasize high-intensity, low-volume work. Example (Bouldering): 2–3 hard bouldering sessions with long rest (limit boulders, circuits), 2–3 hangboard/campus sessions, 1–2 easy climbs for recovery, plus antagonist and core work. Example (Sport): 2 gym strength sessions (heavy bouldering/pull-ups), 2–3 endurance sessions (4×4 circuits or hard route intervals), with sufficient rest.  

**Power-Endurance Week:** Mix of anaerobic workouts and active rest. For example: 2–3 ×4×4 boulder circuits (or laps on a pumpy route), 1–2 easy climbing days (2–3 grades below max), 1 hangboard session (light repeaters), 1–2 rest days.  

**Taper Week:** Only 1–2 short sessions at full intensity (e.g. 1 boulder session at near-max, 1 route crag day), otherwise very easy. Volume typically cut 40–60%. Emphasize sleep, nutrition, and mobility. 

**Recovery/Rest Week:** Scheduled every 3–6 weeks: reduce all training to 40–60% (e.g. 2–3 easy climbs per week, no intense hangs). Use low-impact cross-training (light jog, swimming) to flush fatigue. Steve Bechtel recommends focusing on sleep, hydration, nutrition, and light activity on rest days【37†L79-L87】.

## 4. Exercises & Drills

- **Warm-Up:** ~20+ min total. Begin with full-body movement (jog, bike), joint rotations, light cardio; then climbing-specific prep (easy routes or traverses). Ondra emphasizes a synovial phase (joint lubrication) and a fascial phase (dynamic mobility) before exertion【21†L26-L34】. End with progressive finger/wrist warm-ups (e.g. gentle hangs on large holds, light campus moves).  
- **Antagonist/Accessory:** Essential for balance and injury prevention【23†L34-L43】. Typical exercises:  
  - *Push-ups (20–30 reps ×3):* Builds chest/triceps, stabilizes shoulders (climbers’ classic antagonist)【23†L117-L123】.  
  - *Rows into “cactus” (10–15×3):* Strengthens scapular retractors and rotator cuffs【23†L134-L143】.  
  - *Wrist extensor-ups (20–30×3):* Prevents finger injuries by balancing forearm muscles【23†L99-L107】.  
  - *Farmer’s carries (20–30 steps ×3):* Loads vertical posture muscles (core/shoulders) opposing climbing pull【23†L154-L160】.  
  - *Core drills:* Planks (60–90 s ×3), hanging leg raises (3×10), L-sits (3×10 s), side planks.  
  - *Flexibility/mobility:* Emphasize hip flexors, shoulders, wrists (e.g. banded stretches, yoga).  

- **Fingerboard/Hangboard:** Train specific grips with strict form【29†L246-L254】. Protocols:  
  - *“Minimum Edge” (Beginner/Strength Focus):* 5 hangs ×12 s on the smallest edge you can hold, with 3 min rest between hangs【26†L130-L139】. After five hangs, rest 5 min and repeat for a second set (5 hangs)【26†L130-L139】. Work mainly half-crimp and open-hand grips.  
  - *7s/3s Repeaters (Strength-Endurance):* Select 3–7 grip types. Do sets of 6 repeats: 7 s hang, 3 s rest (≈1 min per set)【29†L231-L239】. Rest 3 min between sets. Add weight so each 7s hang is near-maximal. 1–3 sets per grip.  
  - *Max-Weight Hangs:* Use larger holds (14–20 mm) plus added weight. Do 5–10 s hangs (with 3+ min rest) or 7 s hangs with 53 s rest (the “7-53” protocol)【29†L144-L153】【29†L196-L204】. Focus on peak finger strength under load. 
  - *Safety/Technique:* Always keep shoulders engaged (“shoulders down, chest out”), avoid dead-hangs. Do **no full thumb-lock crimps**, and never train to tendon failure【29†L246-L254】. Limit fingerboard to 1–2×/week max and never back-to-back with climbing. 

- **Campus Board:** Advanced power tool. Use cautiously (injury risk is high【20†L94-L102】). Benefit: explosive contact strength and power【20†L94-L102】. Key exercises:  
  - *Ladders (#1):* Ascend/descend rungs alternately (contact precision).  
  - *Single-Arm Bumps (#2) & Touches (#3):* Practice controlled one-arm moves and precision.  
  - *Jump-Catch (#4):* From matched start, jump to highest reachable rung and catch (develops explosive power)【20†L154-L161】.  
  - *Double Campus “1-5-9” (#5):* Campus one hand 1→5, other 5→9 without matching (benchmark).  
  - *Single-Arm Max (#6):* Max-distance campus with one arm.  
  - *Protocol:* 3–6 reps of an exercise, then 2–5 min rest. Keep strict form (shoulders retracted) to avoid injury【20†L94-L102】. Start with large rungs; progress to smaller holds. Warm up thoroughly and limit sessions to ~20–30 min total. 

- **Limit Bouldering:** Short, very hard problems (~V-grade max). Typically 3–5 problems per session, working 3–5 attempts each, with 3–5 minutes rest between tries. Do 2–3 sets of such problems per session. This builds maximal contact strength and power. (No specific citation, but this is standard practice).

- **ARC (Aerobic Climbing):** Long-duration easy climbing for capillary development. Climb continuously at very low intensity (just above pump threshold) for 20–60+ minutes (often in sets with rest). Steve House’s guidelines: 2×30 min ARC reps plus ~90–120 min of easy route climbing per session, progressively increasing to ~125–180 min in later weeks【53†L189-L198】. Focus on smooth technique and minimal effort per move. Ideal for sport climbers’ base phase; on easy grades well below max.

- **Interval Routes (Power-Endurance):** Climb at ~anaerobic threshold by doing laps on steep routes with fixed rests (e.g. 4 on/4 off, or 6 on/6 off). Common workout: *4×4s* – pick a hard boulder (or steep route), do 4 climbs (staying on rope or bouldering) with ~4 min rest between climbs; repeat 3–4 sets【54†L0-L4】. Alternatively, *repeaters* on a pumpy route (e.g. 5c) with 30 s on/30 s off for 15–20 min. These mimic continuous climbing fatigue and build lactic tolerance.

- **Strength Exercises:**  
  - *Weighted Pull-Ups:* 3–5 reps ×3–5 sets with added weight (progress as able). Develops upper-body pulling power.  
  - *Lock-Off Drills:* Hang at 90° elbow on hold for time (e.g. 5–10 s ×3 each arm) to build mid-position strength.  
  - *Core Drills:* L-Sits on rings or bar (10–30 s ×3), hanging knee raises, planks. A strong core supports static positions and body tension.  
  - All S&C sessions should follow warm-up, target specific weaknesses, and avoid excessive volume (stop before failure, especially shoulders).

**Safety Notes:** Always warm up fully (prevents injury more than post-stretching【21†L26-L34】). Include antagonist exercises to balance musculature【23†L34-L43】. Progress gradually; never max out on fingers or shoulders when fatigued. On fingerboard and campus, emphasize tension in shoulders/traps and proper shoulder blade positioning【29†L246-L254】.

## 5. Testing & Metrics

Climbers should periodically test key metrics to gauge progress (approximately every 6–8 weeks). Common tests include:  
- **Max Boulder Grade:** Attempt an indoor boulder at or above your limit (the highest grade you can do cleanly); improvement is a primary performance indicator.  
- **Route Climbing:** Try an onsight or redpoint of a route near your limit. Times on a fixed boulder circuit (e.g. 4×4s) can also be tracked.  
- **Strength Tests:** 1RM weighted pull-up (or maximal hang weight). Isometric hangs on a 20 mm edge for as long as possible (aim for >10 s). Single-arm hangs if safe.  
- **Fingerboard Protocols:** Repeaters count (e.g. how many reps of 7s/3s at bodyweight) or weight required to hang 7 s on a 20 mm edge. The Horst protocols (7-53) themselves can be used as a test.  
- **Endurance Tests:** Forearm repeaters (e.g. 40 s on/20 s off until failure) or maintaining a pumpy route/laps. Some coaches use lactate threshold tests, but typically measuring steady climbing heart rate or RPE (e.g. keep climbing at a fixed sub-max route until exhaustion).  
- **Other:** Flexibility benchmarks (e.g. pancake stretch), core endurance (plank time), etc.  

One study tested climbers with isometric pull-ups (on jugs vs small edge), finger-crimp hangs, and an intermittent forearm test; they found lead-focused training improved forearm endurance while boulder-focused training improved finger strength, with no loss in the non-targeted discipline【6†L79-L88】【6†L91-L96】.  

Use of **zones** (perceived exertion) can standardize intensity: e.g. 80% effort = “endurance” zone, 90% = “power-endurance,” 100% = “max strength.” TrainingPeaks-style HR zones can guide aerobic sessions (climb at 60–75% max HR for endurance) though labs are rare in climbing. 

## 6. Injury Prevention & Recovery

- **Warm-Up & Mobility:** Always start sessions with full warm-ups (Ondra: synovial lubrication + fascial mobility)【21†L26-L34】. This greatly reduces injury risk.  
- **Antagonist & Stabilizer Training:** Strengthening opposing muscles prevents imbalances. For example, training triceps, mid/lower traps, and rotator cuffs can alleviate common tendon strains【23†L34-L43】. (Climbing.com suggests that focusing on the entire fascial line of the forearm/arm reduces “climber’s elbow”【23†L34-L43】.)  
- **Balanced Programming:** Avoid excessive focus on one quality. Even boulderers should include some endurance, and vice versa, to prevent plateaus and overuse injuries【42†L198-L202】.  
- **Rest & Recovery:** Schedule regular easy weeks (deloads). Steve Bechtel highlights recovery habits: prioritize sleep, eat and hydrate well, use light cardio and nap if needed, and even tactics like cold showers or a “recovery beer” for psychological benefit【37†L79-L87】.  
- **Injury Protocol:** At first sign of pain (e.g. finger pulley or tendon soreness), reduce load and incorporate antagonist/stretching therapy. Consult a professional if pain persists.  

## 7. Sample Plans for Climbers

**Intermediate Boulder Climber (3-6 months):** Train ~3–5 days/week. Example 12-week cycle: *Weeks 1–4 (Endurance/Base):* Moderate bouldering + ARC, general fitness, basic hangboard. *Weeks 5–8 (Strength/Power):* 3–4 hard boulder sessions (limit problems), 2 hangboard or campus sessions, 1 easy climb; plus core/antagonist work. *Weeks 9–11 (Power-Endurance):* 4×4 circuits, circuits of short routes (2–3min climbs), minimal easy climbs. *Week 12 (Peak/Taper):* 1–2 days of short, hard bouldering or redpoint attempts, rest of days easy or off. (Climbing volume ~1.5–3 hours/day, RPE zones 1–3). Goals: improve V-grade, contact strength, and linking ability【48†L88-L95】.  

**Advanced Boulder Climber:** Train ~4–6 days/week (4–5 h/day including rest). Similar phase lengths, but with higher intensity: double sessions possible (e.g. campus + hangboard in one day). Use strict protocols (7-53 hangs, double sessions, greater weight on pull-ups). Prioritize finger recovery (≥2 rest days/week). Peak timing: align hard block to comp or major boulder trip.  

**Intermediate Sport Climber:** Train ~3–5 days/week. Example 12-week: *Weeks 1–6 (Base):* 2–3 ARC climbs of 30–60 min, 1 easy long route session (50–70 moves at 60–70% effort)【53†L189-L198】, plus general fitness. *Weeks 7–10 (Power-Endurance/Strength):* 4×4 boulder sessions, interval routes (6 on/2 off), mixed with 1 strength session (weighted pull-ups, limit boulders). *Weeks 11–12 (Taper):* Two high-intensity days (project routes, max pump problems) then taper volume. Workload ~2–4 h/day including warm-up. Goals: increase redpoint grade, improve endurance on long pitches.  

**Advanced Sport Climber:** Train ~4–6 days/week. Emphasize precise periodization: longer base (up to 12w), multiple interval sessions/week, threshold training. E.g.: 8w base (ARC, high-volume routes), 4w intense VO2max/anaerobic (short steep routes, campus), 4w power-endurance (4×4s, Linked boulders), then taper 1–2w. Include maintenance limit boulders (for power) throughout. As Horst notes, advanced plans target **elite-level** strength/power and endurance concurrently【48†L103-L111】.
