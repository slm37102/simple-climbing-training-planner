Foundations

Principles for the climber who does both

Training boulder and sport at once is the defining constraint at this level. The two ends pull in different directions — short maximal force versus sustained output — and you can't peak both at the exact same time. The plan below resolves that by sequencing them rather than blending them every week.

1. Climbing is the main event; training is the supplement

For a V4–V6 / 6c–7a climber, the largest gains still come from climbing volume and movement skill, not from the gym. Finger strength is necessary but not sufficient at this band — Lattice's ability-level analysis shows its predictive weight relative to technique and other factors shifts as you progress, and it is not the sole lever for an intermediate.[27] Structured strength work is the seasoning, not the meal.

2. Finger strength is the trainable trait that matters most physiologically

Among measurable physical traits, climbing-specific finger strength is the one most strongly tied to performance. In a peer-reviewed study of male climbers, maximal isometric finger strength (a max hang on a ~22 mm edge) correlated r = 0.89 with bouldering and r = 0.67 with redpoint sport grade (both p < 0.01).[25] Peer-reviewed That is why a 20 mm max hang anchors the assessment in §10 — but see the caveats: correlation isn't a recipe, and ~50% of grade variance is unexplained.[28]

3. One priority per block

You make a quality the focus of a phase and let the other discipline tick over in maintenance (one short session a week is usually enough to hold an adaptation while you build something else).[2] Chasing max power and max endurance in the same week is how intermediates plateau.

4. Progressive overload, then recover before you have to

Adaptations come from loading a little harder over weeks, then deliberately backing off. The deload is not a reward for being tired — it is scheduled before you break, typically every fourth week (see §2).[6]

5. Manage fingers like a long-term asset

The most common injury at this level is a finger pulley strain.[22] Fingers adapt far slower than muscle, so the protocols here cap intensity jumps and rotate grips on purpose. Patience on the fingerboard is a performance strategy, not just caution.

The three models — and which to use

Climbing borrows three periodization models from strength sport. They are not equally suited to an intermediate.[2]

ModelHow emphasis changesBest forTrade-off
Linear (classic)One quality at a time, in sequence: endurance → strength → power → power-endurance → peak.Most intermediates, and first structured seasons. This is the RCTM model.[1]Qualities trained early can fade by peak if not maintained.
Undulating (non-linear)Different qualities hit within the same week, varying day to day.Time-crunched climbers; keeping multiple traits alive at once.[2]Harder to drive a true peak; needs good auto-regulation.
Block / conjugateConcentrated blocks overload one system hard, maintain the rest.Advanced climbers with a clear weakness to target.[2]Easy to over-stress one system; less margin for error.
Verified · the coaching taxonomy

One widely-used framing (TrainingBeta) splits periodization into three families — sequential (which contains the classic linear method), concurrent, and conjugate — and notes that in sequential/linear work each cycle develops one quality and runs ~3–4 weeks (2–6 week range), moving from high-volume/low-intensity to low-volume/high-intensity.[2] ✓ Verified

Recommendation

For a V4–V6 / 6c–7a climber doing both disciplines: follow the linear phase arc, but maintain the off-emphasis qualities instead of dropping them — emphasize one quality per block (strength → power → power-endurance) while keeping the others alive with a weekly maintenance touch. In TrainingBeta's taxonomy that's a conjugate approach, which they explicitly recommend for intermediates; the fully concurrent "train everything hard at once" model is reserved for advanced climbers who know their recovery.[2] ✓ Verified You keep the clean peak of a sequence without letting your second discipline rot — and practitioners widely warn that rigid one-quality-at-a-time plans over-structure real climbers.[5]

Deloads — the non-negotiable

A deload is a planned easy week that lets accumulated fatigue clear so adaptation can surface. The standard cadence is 3 loading weeks + 1 reduced week.[6] The climbing-specific detail that trips people up:

Deload = cut volume, hold intensity

Reduce the amount of work (roughly 40–50% fewer hard sets / climbs), but keep the quality high — a few crisp, heavy efforts. Don't turn the hard edges soft; just do fewer of them. This preserves the strength stimulus while dropping the fatigue.

Peaking & tapering

To send hard on a known date, schedule backwards from it. The last phase is a taper: 1–2 weeks of much-reduced volume but maintained intensity, so you arrive fresh and strong rather than fried.[3] Peak power-endurance and limit power are perishable — they fade in ~2–3 weeks — so they're trained last and deliberately, then held with minimal touches through the taper. The aerobic base built in Phase 1 is far more durable and needs almost no maintenance to persist.[4]

The arc

The macrocycle, phase by phase

This is the canonical climbing sequence — the Anderson brothers' RCTM, a sequential/linear periodization with four named phases in fixed order, Base Fitness → Strength → Power → Power Endurance, followed by a Performance/Peak window and then Rest.[1] ✓ Verified Virtually every other framework uses the same phases, just renamed.[3] Read it top to bottom: each phase builds the substrate the next one exploits.

Reading the arc

Notice the shift in both intensity and energy system: long-and-easy at the start (build the engine and the tendons), short-and-heavy in the middle (build force), then medium-hard-and-repeated near the end (learn to apply force when pumped), then sharp-and-fresh to perform. Strength is trained before power for a reason — you can't express power you don't have, and power work on under-prepared fingers is where injuries cluster.

Balancing boulder & sport across the arc. The discipline emphasis tracks the phase. The Strength and Power phases lean bouldering (short, maximal). The Power-endurance phase leans sport (sustained). Throughout, the non-emphasised discipline gets one maintenance session a week. If your goal event is a boulder trip, shorten Phase 4; if it's a sport redpoint, extend it.

The microcycle

Weekly templates

Three to four climbing/training days a week is the sweet spot at this level — enough stimulus, enough recovery. Hard finger days need ~48 h between them.[7] The hard rule for sequencing a session: skill → power → strength → endurance → conditioning. Do the most neurally demanding, least fatigued work first.

Verified · how much do strong climbers actually train?

Lattice's member data is reassuring: a V4 climber averages ~3 training days/week, a V12+ only ~4 — about one extra day at the elite end. And total weekly exercise volume is nearly flat (~22 items/week) across every grade band. The lever at the top isn't more volume — it's quality, consistency and intensity.[35] ✓ Verified

Protocol · the engine room

Fingerboard protocols

The fingerboard is the most efficient and most dangerous tool you have. The standard benchmark edge is 20 mm (about one finger-pad deep), and the two grips to live on are half-crimp and open-hand / open-crimp.[10] Peer-reviewed Beginners-to-intermediates should use two-arm hangs — load spreads across the shoulder girdle and setup is simpler.[9]

Max HangsHigh / maxPeer-reviewed

Pure finger strength — heavy, short, fully recovered. The "deadlift for fingers."[8] Trained in the Strength phase.

Verified dose-response

Three brief hangboard sessions a week, for four weeks, produces a noticeable finger-strength increase — especially for those newer to hangboarding — and for intermediates, a few multi-week cycles bring clear gains.[10] Peer-reviewed You do not need marathon sessions; you need consistent, progressive, well-recovered ones.

Finger safety

Never hangboard cold — climb or do light hangs first. Stop a hang the instant form fails or you feel a sharp finger sensation. Limit full crimp on the board; bias half-crimp and open-hand, which load the pulleys less aggressively.[23] Add load in small increments — fingers adapt slower than the muscles that want to pull harder.

Upper-body pulling strength supports steep climbing and lock-offs. It's trained alongside max hangs in the Strength phase.

Lock-offs & uneven pullsModerateExpert framework

Position-specific strength for holding a hand still while the other reaches. Hold lock-offs at 90° and 120° elbow angles; progress to uneven-grip pull-ups (one hand high, one low) as a step toward one-arm strength.[19]

Contact strength & core

Contact strength — the ability to generate grip force instantly on a hold — is trained on the wall through limit bouldering and (carefully) campus work rather than on the board.[24] Core is trained as tension, not crunches: front levers/progressions, hanging leg raises, planks, and the body-tension you build by climbing steep terrain with precise feet. 2–3 short core sets after climbing, most training days.

Protocol · force, fast

Power & limit work

Power is force applied quickly — the explosive, dynamic end of climbing. It sits on top of the strength you built in the previous phase.

Campus BoardHigh / maxExpert framework
Intermediate caution

Campusing is high-risk for fingers and elbows and is easy to start too early. Build a finger-strength base first. A safer substitute that hits the same anaerobic-power quality is "boulder campusing" — repeating a hard boulder problem powerfully — which Hörst recommends as a gentler on-ramp.[16]

If you do campus: basic ladders (matched, then 1-3-5) on large rungs, fully warmed up, low volume, long rests, and only in the Power phase. Skip it entirely if you have any elbow or finger niggles.

Staying in the game

Injury prevention & antagonists

Climbing is overwhelmingly a pulling, finger-flexing sport. Left unchecked, that imbalance is what eventually hurts you. Three habits cover most of the risk.

Warm up in a ramp, never cold

Pulse-raise → mobility → easy climbing → progressively harder, finishing with a brief dynamic rotator-cuff activation before anything maximal.[21] Warm-up is most critical before hard projecting and any fingerboarding.[23]

Train the antagonists

Balance the pulling with pushing and extension so forces across the joints stay even.[20]

AreaWhyDo
Pushing musclesBalance pull-dominance (triceps, chest, mid-delt)Push-ups / dips, shoulder press, band pushes — 2×/week
Finger & wrist extensorsCounter the flexor load on the pulleysReverse wrist curls, rubber-band finger extensions
Rotator cuffShoulder stability climbers rarely trainShort cuff routine (internal/external rotation) — ~10 min, 2–3×/week[21]

Protect the pulleys

Finger pulley sprains are the signature intermediate injury.[22] The prevention checklist, straight from the Rock Rehab approach:

  • Avoid sudden dynamic loading on small crimps when cold or tired.
  • Don't over-rely on full crimp — rotate to half-crimp and 3-finger drag to spread load across grips.[22]
  • Watch the load spike — keep total weekly stress from outrunning recovery; this is exactly what deloads protect against.[23]
  • Respect tweaks. A graded return after a sprain (gradual reloading, grip variation) beats pushing through.[22]
Evidence & metrics

Assessment & benchmarks

Testing turns training from guesswork into a feedback loop. The headline metric for climbers is the max hang on a 20 mm edge, expressed as added load relative to bodyweight.[30]

The core test

Test
Maximum added weight for a ~7–10 s hang on a 20 mm edge, half-crimp, two arms[29]
Express as
(bodyweight + added) ÷ bodyweight, or simply track added kg over time
Cadence
Retest at phase boundaries / every 4–6 weeks, fresh and warmed up

What the evidence actually supports

  • It predicts performance. Finger strength is the physical trait most strongly tied to grade — r = 0.89 for bouldering, r = 0.67 for redpoint in male climbers.[25] Peer-reviewed
  • The test is reliable. A 2025 systematic review (15 studies, 747 participants) found max isometric finger-strength tests reliable — 14 of 15 studies showed high reliability (ICC > 0.75) and 12 showed very high reliability (ICC 0.85–0.99), median ICC 0.86.[26][31] Peer-reviewed
  • Use a ~20 mm fixed edge. That review found assessments on fixed-depth edges of ~20–23 mm consistently show high reliability and should be prioritized for standardized monitoring — directly validating a 20 mm max-hang as the number to track.[31] Peer-reviewed
  • But it isn't standardized. Studies used edges from 6–60 mm and different grips, so reliability ranged widely (ICC 0.294–1) and a meta-analysis wasn't even possible.[26] Peer-reviewed The review calls for load cells and harmonized protocols.[26]
✗ Refuted by the fact-check

A tempting claim — that bodyweight bent-arm hang tests are simply unreliable for intermediates and should be abandoned for a load cell — was refuted 0–3 by the adversarial reviewers. The evidence supports fixed-edge isometric tests as the most reliable, but does not condemn a consistently-performed self-test. Pick one protocol and standardize it.[31]

Read this before chasing a number

Cross-person %-bodyweight charts are shakier than they look. Because edge size, hang time and grip aren't standardized, a "%BW by grade" table from one source isn't comparable to another.[26] A community model of Lattice's 20 mm dataset explains only ~50% of grade variance (r ≈ 0.70, n ≈ 900) — finger strength is a strong signal, not a verdict.[28] The reliable use is intra-individual: track your own 20 mm max hang over time and train to move it. That's where the evidence is solid.

How to adapt it

Goal is bouldering? Extend Strength/Power (wk 5–10) and shrink the PE block. Only 3 days/week? Drop the maintenance touches first, never the phase's main session. Feeling beaten up before a scheduled deload? Take it early — the calendar serves the body, not the reverse.

Honesty

Caveats & open questions

  • Coaching consensus ≠ controlled trials. The phase structure, session designs and most numbers here are strong practitioner consensus (Hörst, Lattice, RCTM, Vagy), not all confirmed by RCTs. They're reliable starting points, not proven optima.
  • Finger-strength testing isn't standardized. Edge size, hang duration and grip vary across sources, so absolute %BW benchmarks aren't comparable between protocols.[26] Pick one protocol and stick to it.
  • Most performance variance is unexplained. Finger strength predicts grade strongly but leaves ~50% unexplained;[28] technique, tactics, head-game and mobility fill the gap — especially at the intermediate band.[27]
  • The research skews to men and to bouldering. The strongest correlation figures here come from a male-only study;[25] sport/redpoint and female-specific data are thinner.
  • Linear vs. undulating is genuinely debated. Practitioners disagree on how rigidly intermediates should periodize; the hybrid recommended here is a pragmatic middle, not a settled answer.[5]
  • Open question — optimal hangboard dose for intermediates. "Noticeable gains in a few cycles" is verified,[10] but the best long-run frequency, load and edge progression for this specific band isn't pinned down by the literature.
  • Not everything was verified. 31 claims passed the adversarial fact-check, but the deep gather surfaced ~300 claims total — the remaining ~270 (held in the project's claim store) are well-sourced but not individually re-checked, and appear here only where they match expert-framework consensus. The fact-check itself is three skeptical AI reviewers, not ground truth.
