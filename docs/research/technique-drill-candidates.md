# Technique Drill Candidates

> Candidate list for [wayfinder ticket #15](https://github.com/slm37102/simple-climbing-training-planner/issues/15)
> (map [#14](https://github.com/slm37102/simple-climbing-training-planner/issues/14): expanded technique-drill
> library). **Deliberately over-generated** — 18 candidates across 5 categories; a follow-up human-in-the-loop
> grilling ticket cuts this down to the final `SKILL_DRILLS` list. Audience: one intermediate climber
> (V5–V6 boulder / ~7a lead, 7b lead goal), 3 years' experience, boulder + sport.
>
> Training information only — **not medical advice.** Drill descriptions are drawn from the cited coaching
> sources; where evidence is thin or anecdotal this is said rather than the drill dropped.

**Per-drill fields**

- **Focus** — 1–3 sentences of direct athlete instruction, in the voice of the existing `SKILL_DRILLS`
  entries in `js/program.js`.
- **Contexts** — `tuesday` (dedicated light-day practice, may need setup/spotter) and/or `warmup`
  (quick habit doable during the 4–6 easy warm-up boulders on a Thu/Sat main session).
- **Finger-neutral?** — all drills must be doable on jugs / easy terrain without near-max finger loading;
  risks are flagged, not hidden.

**Existing drills (do not duplicate):** silent feet · flagging · quiet hands (straight-arm resting /
minimal grip time) · falling practice (lead).

## 1. Footwork & balance

At V5–V6, sloppy feet is the classic residual habit: Dave MacLeod calls poor foot weighting the single
most common error that shifts load onto the upper body and caps move difficulty
([davemacleod.com](https://www.davemacleod.com/shop/9outof10climbers), via
[Gripped's summary](https://gripped.com/indoor-climbing/correct-your-footwork-bad-habits-with-climbing-pro-dave-macleod/)).

### 1.1 Target practice

- **Focus:** Before each foot move, pick the single best spot on the hold, then place your toe on that
  exact spot without taking your eyes off it. Keep the foot still once placed — move your body, not
  your foot.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday`, `warmup`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — done on climbs ~a grade below onsight level; hands stay easy.
- **Source:** Eric Hörst, [Improve Climbing Footwork with "Target Practice"](https://trainingforclimbing.com/improve-climbing-footwork-with-target-practice/) (primary).

### 1.2 Downclimbing

- **Focus:** After topping out an easy climb, downclimb it on the same holds. Going down forces you to
  look for feet, weight them precisely, and control your hips — and doubles the value of every lap.
- **Contexts:** `warmup` (downclimb your warm-up boulders), `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes on jugs/easy terrain, but it **roughly doubles time-under-tension per lap** —
  keep it 2+ grades below max and stop before forearms load up, especially on a light day.
- **Source:** Eric Hörst, [Effective Gym Training Strategies for Route Climbing](https://trainingforclimbing.com/effective-gym-training-strategies-for-route-climbing/) (primary).

### 1.3 Tracking (feet follow hands)

- **Focus:** Climb an easy route where your feet may only use the exact holds your hands used. Forces
  high steps, hand-foot matches, and balanced stand-ups you'd otherwise avoid.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday` (needs an eliminate mindset and route choice), `warmup` possible on very easy terrain
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — Hörst prescribes routes 2–3 grades below max; hands stay on easy holds.
- **Source:** Eric Hörst, [Effective Gym Training Strategies for Route Climbing](https://trainingforclimbing.com/effective-gym-training-strategies-for-route-climbing/) (primary).

### 1.4 Edge pivoting

- **Focus:** Place the inside edge of your big toe on a hold, then pivot round so the outside edge takes
  the same hold, without lifting off. Start on big holds and shrink the hold as it gets smooth.
- **Contexts:** `warmup`, `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — doable standing on a low traverse or big footholds with jug hands.
- **Source:** Lattice Training, [Rock Climbing Footwork: The Basics](https://latticetraining.com/blog/rock-climbing-footwork-the-basics/) (primary).

## 2. Body positioning & silhouette

Hip position is where intermediates leak the most energy on steep ground; turning the hip in adds
6–10 in of reach and moves weight from arms to feet
([Climbing magazine](https://www.climbing.com/skills/hips-climbing-training/);
[PitchSix hip-rotation drill](https://pitchsix.com/blogs/academy/academy-hip-rotation-drill)).

### 2.1 Turn-and-reach (same-side hip rotation)

- **Focus:** Every time you reach, rotate the same-side hip into the wall first, then extend off a
  straight arm. Feel the reach get longer without pulling harder.
- **Contexts:** `warmup`, `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — practiced on easy terrain; the point is the hip, not the hold.
- **Source:** [PitchSix Academy: Hip Rotation Drill](https://pitchsix.com/blogs/academy/academy-hip-rotation-drill); reinforced by Neil Gresham's Masterclass steep-wall segments ([UKC series index](https://www.ukclimbing.com/articles/skills/series/neil_gresham_technique_and_training/)).

### 2.2 Open vs. closed hips (compare laps)

- **Focus:** Climb the same easy route twice: once square to the wall with hips open and frogged, once
  with hips closed using backsteps and drop-knees on every move. Exaggerate both; notice which moves
  each style makes cheap.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday` (needs two deliberate laps on one route)
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — same easy route both laps.
- **Source:** [Climbing magazine, "Struggling With Powerful Climbs? Start Training Your Hips"](https://www.climbing.com/skills/hips-climbing-training/). Comparative-laps format is coaching convention rather than studied protocol — thin evidence, strong face validity.

### 2.3 Backstep & drop-knee ladder

- **Focus:** On an easy overhanging route, backstep or drop-knee every single move — no square-on
  climbing allowed. Keep hips sucked into the wall and arms long.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday`; `warmup` only if the gym's warm-up terrain includes gentle overhang
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes on juggy overhang; steep terrain raises effort, so keep the grade well down.
- **Source:** Neil Gresham, Masterclass Part 1 (steep-wall technique; [YouTube series](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLBCRwO0FN0zMTqSfFW9SMbK2tncTrI25r)); twist/backstep fundamentals in [Lattice's movement-concepts post](https://latticetraining.com/blog/learn-100-climbing-techniques-with-just-4-movement-concepts/).

### 2.4 Straight-arm traverse

- **Focus:** Traverse or climb easy ground keeping your arms straight the whole time — move by shifting
  hips and bending legs, never by bending elbows. If an elbow bends, back up and redo the move.
- **Contexts:** `warmup`, `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — jug traverse; complements the existing "quiet hands" (static straight-arm
  rests) by making straight arms the default *while moving*.
- **Source:** [Vertical Horizons, "Climbing with Straight Arms"](https://www.verticalhorizonsclimbinggym.com/post/climbing-with-straight-arms); a staple of Gresham's "precise feet, straight arms, relaxed grip, fluid moves, pace" drill loop ([Climbing magazine byline](https://www.climbing.com/byline/neil-gresham/)).

## 3. Route-reading & beta

Reading before pulling on is the cheapest grade upgrade available to a 7a→7b leader: time on the ground
is energy saved on the route, and planned sequences climb faster and more fluidly
([Climbing magazine, "Reading Routes"](https://www.climbing.com/skills/reading-routes/)).

### 3.1 Ground-up read & mime

- **Focus:** Before pulling on, read the whole line: pick every handhold, mime the hand sequence with
  your eyes on the route, and note where the crux is. Climb it, then compare what actually happened
  with your plan.
- **Contexts:** `warmup` (read every warm-up climb before starting it), `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — the drill is done standing on the ground; climbing part is easy terrain.
- **Source:** [Climbing magazine, "Increase Your Onsight Grade By Reading Routes From the Ground"](https://www.climbing.com/skills/reading-routes/); same protocol at [La Fabrique Verticale](https://lafabriqueverticale.com/en/increase-your-onsight-grade-by-reading-routes-from-the-ground/).

### 3.2 First touch

- **Focus:** Use every handhold exactly as you first touch it — no readjusting, no regripping. Wrong
  grab? Live with it to the next hold. Punishes lazy reading and teaches you to grab right first time.
- **Contexts:** `warmup`, `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes on easy terrain; a bad first touch on a hard climb could load fingers
  awkwardly, so keep it 2+ grades down.
- **Source:** Eric Hörst, [Effective Gym Training Strategies for Route Climbing](https://trainingforclimbing.com/effective-gym-training-strategies-for-route-climbing/) (primary).

### 3.3 Rest-spotting read

- **Focus:** Before a route, identify from the ground every rest and clipping stance you expect to use —
  then verify each one on the way up. Were they real? What did you miss?
- **Contexts:** `tuesday` (route/lead-oriented), `warmup` on rope days
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — reading exercise on easy routes.
- **Source:** [Trail & Crag, "How to Rest on Route"](https://trailandcrag.com/rock-climbing/how-to-find-rests); rest/clip-stance identification is also step one of the visualization protocol in [Climbing's route-reading article](https://www.climbing.com/skills/reading-routes/).

## 4. Pacing & resting on the wall

For a 7a leader chasing 7b, recovery-on-route is often worth more than extra strength: making the most
of every rest is what lets you arrive at the crux able to try
([Trail & Crag](https://trailandcrag.com/rock-climbing/how-to-find-rests)).

### 4.1 G-tox shakeout

- **Focus:** At every rest, alternate shaking each arm overhead and hanging at your side, 5–10 seconds
  per position. Gravity drains the pump faster than the usual dangle-only shake.
- **Contexts:** `warmup` (build the habit on easy mileage), `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — done at rests on jugs.
- **Source:** Eric Hörst's G-Tox, described at [Trail & Crag](https://trailandcrag.com/rock-climbing/how-to-find-rests); Hörst's own materials at [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/training-articles/). Evidence: one small supporting study plus coaching consensus — modest but real.

### 4.2 Milk every rest

- **Focus:** On an easy route, stop at every rest you spotted and stay until your breathing settles and
  both arms have been shaken out — only then move. Learn what a full reset feels like.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday` (deliberately slow laps)
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — easy terrain, resting is the point.
- **Source:** [Trail & Crag, "How to Rest on Route"](https://trailandcrag.com/rock-climbing/how-to-find-rests); Gresham's Masterclass Part 2 covers resting and pace change on lead ([overview](https://www.neilgresham.com/coaching-and-training/group-masterclasses)).

### 4.3 Pace shifting

- **Focus:** Match your speed to the terrain: attack hard sections fast and decisive, then deliberately
  slow down, relax your grip, and breathe on easy ground. Practice flipping between the two modes on
  one climb.
- **Contexts:** `warmup`, `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes on easy terrain.
- **Source:** Neil Gresham's pacing doctrine — "attack when it's hard, defend when it's easy" ([masterclass overview](https://www.neilgresham.com/coaching-and-training/group-masterclasses), [UKC series](https://www.ukclimbing.com/articles/skills/series/neil_gresham_technique_and_training/)).

### 4.4 Continuous breathing

- **Focus:** Climb an easy route breathing steadily and audibly the whole way — exhale on hard pulls.
  If you catch yourself holding your breath, pause, reset with one slow breath, and continue.
- **Contexts:** `warmup`, `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes.
- **Source:** breath-control-while-climbing appears across [Climbing magazine's mental-training pieces](https://www.climbing.com/skills/learn-this-mental-training-for-climbers-2/) and [Red Bull's mental-training roundup](https://www.redbull.com/us-en/mental-training-techniques-for-climbing); part of Gresham's drill loop ("…relaxed grip, fluid moves, pace"). Evidence anecdotal/coaching consensus; low cost.

## 5. Mental & composure

MacLeod's core claim: for most self-coached climbers the mind — hesitation, fear of falling, bailing
early — is the cheapest big win, ahead of more strength
([9 Out of 10 Climbers](https://www.davemacleod.com/shop/9outof10climbers)). Falling practice (existing
drill) is the anchor here; these extend it.

### 5.1 Pre-climb centering breath

- **Focus:** Before every attempt, stand at the base and box-breathe — in for 4, hold 4, out 4 — until
  your shoulders drop. Same ritual, every climb, so calm becomes automatic on redpoint day.
- **Contexts:** `warmup` (do it before each warm-up climb), `tuesday`
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes — done standing on the ground.
- **Source:** box-breathing pre-climb rituals in [Red Bull's 10 mental training techniques](https://www.redbull.com/us-en/mental-training-techniques-for-climbing) and [Climbing magazine](https://www.climbing.com/skills/learn-this-mental-training-for-climbers-2/). Breathing-for-arousal-control is well supported in general sport psychology; climbing-specific evidence anecdotal.

### 5.2 No-take commitment lap

- **Focus:** Pick an easy lead and ban the word "take": you climb to the anchor or fall trying — no
  grabbing draws, no hanging to think. Decide, then go.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday` only — needs a rope, a belayer, and a fall-safe easy route.
- **Finger-neutral?** Yes physically (route is easy); the load is mental.
- **Source:** commitment/hesitation as the redpoint limiter in [UKC's "Mind Games: The Psychology of Redpointing"](https://www.ukclimbing.com/articles/features/mind_games_the_psychology_of_redpointing-8433) and MacLeod's fear-of-falling chapters ([9 Out of 10 Climbers](https://www.davemacleod.com/shop/9outof10climbers)). Drill format is coaching convention, not a studied protocol.

### 5.3 Pump-panic composure ⚠

- **Focus:** On juggy overhang well below max, climb until properly pumped, then practice staying
  composed: slow the breathing, down-grade the gripping, find the next rest — no panic-grabbing or
  bailing. Fear plus resistance is panic; fear alone is manageable.
- **Contexts:** `tuesday` — **but see flag.**
- **Finger-neutral?** **Flagged.** Holds are jugs (submax fingers) but the drill *deliberately builds
  deep forearm pump*, which conflicts with Tuesday's light/recovery intent and the finger-neutral
  spirit. Included so the grilling ticket can consciously keep or cut it; if kept it may belong on a
  climbing day, not Tuesday.
- **Source:** fear/panic decoupling in [Climbing magazine's mental-training article](https://www.climbing.com/skills/learn-this-mental-training-for-climbers-2/); pump management on lead in Gresham's Masterclass Part 2 ([overview](https://www.neilgresham.com/coaching-and-training/group-masterclasses)).

## Coverage notes

- **Counts:** footwork 4 · body positioning 4 · route-reading 3 · pacing/resting 4 · mental 3 = **18 candidates**.
- **Not included:** Hörst's *speed training* (laps at +10% pace — a performance protocol, closer to
  power-endurance programming than a pick-one skill drill); eyes-closed/blindfold traverses from the
  Self-Coached Climber tradition ([Hague & Hunter](https://www.amazon.com/Self-Coached-Climber-Movement-Training-Performance/dp/0811733394) — 52-drill catalogue; balance/center-of-gravity framework)
  — high setup awkwardness in a busy gym for marginal gain over Target Practice.
- **Overlap watch:** *straight-arm traverse* borders the existing **quiet hands** (static straight-arm
  rests vs. straight arms while moving); *ground-up read & mime* and *rest-spotting read* could merge
  into one "read before you pull on" drill if the final list needs trimming.
- **Warm-up-suitable subset** (quick habit, no setup): target practice, edge pivoting, downclimbing,
  turn-and-reach, straight-arm traverse, ground-up read & mime, first touch, G-tox shakeout, pace
  shifting, continuous breathing, pre-climb centering breath.
