### Periodization models & macrocycle architecture  (22)

- **Lattice describes block periodization as splitting a macrocycle into mesocycle blocks of roughly 3-12 weeks, each with a single training focus (e.g. a base-building block for muscular endurance, or a dedicated strength-development block for grip/finger strength).**  
  _search · vote 3-0 · [latticetraining.com](https://latticetraining.com/blog/how-to-structure-your-training/)_
  > "Block periodization splits a macrocycle into mesocycle blocks of ~3-12 weeks, each with a single focus (e.g. Base Building for muscular endurance, a strength-development block for grip strength)."

- **Undulating (non-linear) periodization varies the training focus daily/weekly within a single microcycle (e.g. strength Monday, endurance Wednesday, power Friday), in contrast to block periodization's sequential single-focus blocks.**  
  _search · vote 3-0 · [latticetraining.com](https://latticetraining.com/blog/how-to-structure-your-training/)_
  > "Undulating/non-linear alternatively varies focus daily/weekly within a microcycle (strength Mon, endurance Wed, power Fri) and suits in-season climbers and beginners needing a broad base."

- **Lattice frames the core periodization tradeoff as sequence (block: one focus at a time, in order) versus simultaneity (undulating: multiple focuses concurrently within the week), and takes the stance that there is no single best model — it should be chosen by experience level, goal, and available time.**

### Base / aerobic-capacity phase programming  (23)

### Strength phase programming (fingers + pulling)  (18)

- **Hörst's core hypergravity max-strength protocol is 5 sets of 5 weighted pull-ups, performed with 3 minutes of rest between each set.**

  _search · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/hypergravity-pull-up-training-for-increased-strength-power-and-endurance/)_

### Power phase: limit bouldering & campus  (19)

- **Limit bouldering should target maximum effort on short sequences of no more than 3-5 moves (six or fewer hand moves); anything beyond that shifts the stimulus from pure strength/power into power-endurance.**  
  _search · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/limit-bouldering-for-building-max-climbing-strength-and-power/)_
  > "Limit bouldering consists of no more than three to five moves at your absolute limit... anything more than that quickly switches modes from pure strength and power training into power endurance."

- **A limit-bouldering session should last 60-75 minutes of limit and near-limit bouldering and/or system boarding.**  
  _search · vote 2-1 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/limit-bouldering-for-building-max-climbing-strength-and-power/)_
  > "Limit bouldering sessions should be 60-75 minutes of limit and near-limit bouldering and/or system boarding."

- **Rest 3-5 minutes between attempts/goes so every effort is a high-quality, full-power attempt.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/limit-bouldering-for-building-max-climbing-strength-and-power/)_
  > "It's vital to make only high-quality, full-power efforts, so be sure to rest for 3-5 minutes between goes."

- **Limit-bouldering frequency should be capped at roughly 1-2x per week and total limit training/climbing kept to an aggregate of 3-4 days per week; do not limit boulder two days in a row, to avoid injury and overtraining.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/limit-bouldering-for-building-max-climbing-strength-and-power/)_
  > "Limit training and climbing to an aggregate of three or four days per week—any more tempts injury and will likely lead to overtraining syndrome."

- **Quality over fatigue: end the limit-bouldering session before reaching total exhaustion, because fatigue has no place in limit bouldering.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/limit-bouldering-for-building-max-climbing-strength-and-power/)_
  > "Fatigue has no place in limit bouldering. End your bouldering before you reach a state of total exhaustion. If you find yourself reaching this state of high fatigue, then end your session now."

- **Readiness benchmarks before campus boarding: 2-5 years of consistent climbing, V5-V6 / 5.12 ability, 15-20 solid pull-ups, and the ability to do a 1-2-3-4-5 ladder without matching hands (the minimum strength to survive the board's reactive forces).**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/how-to-campus-board/)_
  > "Readiness benchmarks for campus board: 2-5 years consistent climbing, V5-V6 / 5.12 ability, 15-20 solid pull-ups, and ability to do a 1-2-3-4-5 ladder without matching hands (minimum strength to survive the board's reactive forces)."

- **Rest between sets should be a minimum of 2.5 minutes, extending to 3-5 minutes for power work and 5+ minutes for elite athletes.**  
  _reasoning · vote 2-1 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/how-to-campus-board/)_
  > "Emphasize quality, rest between sets (2.5 minutes minimum). For specific training protocols, lead with one arm, rest two or three minutes and repeat, leading with the other arm for one set."

- **Training frequency: first-time campus boarders go once a week, build up to twice a week; elites should campus no more than three times per week. Campus in phases of three to six weeks rather than year-round.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/how-to-campus-board/)_
  > "First-time campus boarders should go once a week, then build up to twice a week. No need for elites to campus more than three times per week. Rather than campus year round, campus in phases of three to six weeks."

- **A campus workout should consist of 2-3 movement types; the best power exercises are max ladders, double dynos, and up-downs.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/how-to-campus-board/)_
  > "Workouts = 2-3 movement types; best power exercises are max ladders, double dynos, and up-downs."

- **Readiness gate before campusing: you must be able to do a 1-2-3-4-5 ladder (moving rung to rung without skipping) without matching hands on any rung; if you cannot, campus board training is not yet recommended.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [gripped.com](https://gripped.com/sections/training/a-beginners-guide-to-campus-board-training/)_
  > "If you cannot do a 1-2-3-4-5 ladder without matching hands on any rung, campusing is NOT yet recommended."

- **Intermediate entry protocol is '2-2-2': 2 sets of 2 reps per exercise, with 2 minutes of rest between sets.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [gripped.com](https://gripped.com/sections/training/a-beginners-guide-to-campus-board-training/)_
  > "Intermediate entry 2-2-2 protocol: 2 sets of 2 reps per exercise with 2 minutes rest between sets."

- **Campus board training is highly strenuous and should be done at most 2-3 times per week; first-timers should start once a week and build up to twice a week, with elites not exceeding three times per week.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [gripped.com](https://gripped.com/sections/training/a-beginners-guide-to-campus-board-training/)_
  > "Campus board training is very strenuous — do it at most 2-3 times per week."

- **Campusing for power is done on the biggest rungs: for the large majority of the workout, athletes stay on the Metolius Large Wooden Rungs (1 1/4" / ~32mm deep), used incut side up. The governing equation is 'Big Rungs + Big Moves = More Power.'**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [powercompanyclimbing.com](https://www.powercompanyclimbing.com/blog/2015/04/campusing-part-1-for-powerbig-rungs-big.html)_
  > "Nearly everyone directed to the campus board, for the large majority of their workout, stays on the biggest rungs (using the Metolius Large Wooden Rungs, which are 1 1/4" deep, incut side up). ... Big Rungs + Big Moves = More Power."

- **The core power exercise starts on a higher rung (3 or 4), drops to a lower rung, and immediately explodes upward; advanced climbers should drop 2 rungs once they have that movement dialed.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [powercompanyclimbing.com](https://www.powercompanyclimbing.com/blog/2015/04/campusing-part-1-for-powerbig-rungs-big.html)_
  > "you'll start on a higher rung (3 or 4), drop to a lower rung (advanced climbers should drop 2 rungs if they have that movement dialed), and immediately explode upward"

- **Contact/ground time on the lower rung must be minimized — the less time in contact with the lower rung, the better — because a too-long transition (amortization) phase dissipates stored elastic energy and degrades the move into a mere explosive pull-up rather than a plyometric.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [powercompanyclimbing.com](https://www.powercompanyclimbing.com/blog/2015/04/campusing-part-1-for-powerbig-rungs-big.html)_
  > "The less time that you are in contact with the lower rung, the better. ... Keeping this phase as short as possible is crucial for power development. If the transition phase takes too long, the stored energy dissipates, and the plyometric effect is lost, making the exercise simply an explosive pullup…"

- **Limit boulder problems should be kept short — roughly 3 to 5 moves — because longer problems become strength-endurance challenges rather than tests of pure power; save long problems for open bouldering or practice sessions.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingbeta.com](https://www.trainingbeta.com/4-keys-to-limit-bouldering/)_
  > "New gyms tend to feature problems that are much longer than the recommended 3 to 5 move guideline, which tend to become strength endurance challenges rather than a question of pure power. Save the long problems for open bouldering or practice sessions."

- **Each attempt should be as close to 100% effort as possible; one quality all-out go builds more power than many moderate-intensity attempts.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingbeta.com](https://www.trainingbeta.com/4-keys-to-limit-bouldering/)_
  > "Your goes need to be as close as possible to 100% effort. One quality effort at 100% is going to do a lot more for your power than a bunch of attempts at a moderate intensity."

- **Rest between attempts scales with effort: ~1 minute is enough while still working out beta, but after an all-out effort you may need 5 minutes or more before the next go.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingbeta.com](https://www.trainingbeta.com/4-keys-to-limit-bouldering/)_
  > "If you're still just figuring out the beta, you probably don't need too much more than a minute rest. If you've given an all-out effort, you may need to rest five minutes or more."

- **Limit sessions should be kept short and stopped once power output drops off — you should avoid getting totally wrecked or pumped, because power cannot be trained effectively when tired and depleted.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingbeta.com](https://www.trainingbeta.com/4-keys-to-limit-bouldering/)_
  > "You should avoid the feeling of being totally wrecked when limit bouldering... Once your power levels drop off, it's better to call it quits, recover, and come back fresh for another session."


### Power-endurance / anaerobic-capacity protocols  (21)

- **Hörst's 60/60 intervals protocol prescribes 60 seconds of moderately hard climbing followed by 60 seconds of rest (a 1:1 climb-to-rest ratio), repeated for roughly 10 to 30 minutes of aggregate climbing-plus-rest time.**  
  _search · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/power-endurance-training-protocols-for-climbers/)_
  > "The 60/60 intervals protocol consists of 60 seconds of moderately hard climbing followed by 60 seconds of rest, maintaining a climb-rest ratio of 1:1 for a total of 10 to 30 minutes of aggregate climbing and rest."

- **The target intensity for 60/60 threshold/aerobic-power interval training is RPE 7 to 8.5 out of 10, hovering at the anaerobic threshold; climbers should stop before entering the major pump zone of 8.5+ to avoid going too anaerobic/lactic.**  
  _search · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/power-endurance-training-protocols-for-climbers/)_
  > "It's essential to maintain a perceived exertion of between a 7 and 8.5 out of 10... End your climbing early rather than climb into the major pump zone of 8.5+ out of 10."

- **Power-endurance interval work-to-rest ratios start around 1:1 and can progress to 1:0.5, with about 4–6 sets per session; the recommended progression is to increase total climbing time first before reducing rest or increasing movement density.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/power-endurance-training-protocols-for-climbers/)_
  > "Work-to-rest ratios start around 1:1 and can progress to 1:0.5, with 4–6 sets per session. The recommended progression is to first increase total climbing time before reducing rest intervals or increasing movement density."

- **Anaerobic-capacity (lactic 'long power endurance') efforts are sustained moderately-high-intensity climbing of up to about 3 minutes; high-anaerobic-capacity climbers can hold powerful pumped sequences for upward of 2 minutes, whereas similarly-skilled climbers with lower capacity may fail in under 60 seconds.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [trainingforclimbing.com](https://trainingforclimbing.com/power-endurance-training-protocols-for-climbers/)_
  > "Individuals with high anaerobic capacity will excel at climbing long, powerful sequences—despite a growing pump—lasting upward of 2 minutes without a rest, whereas a climber of similar technical ability but lesser anaerobic capacity will fail more quickly, perhaps in under 60 seconds."

- **The 4x4 anaerobic-capacity workout uses 4 boulder problems set 2-4 grades below the climber's max (e.g. a V8 boulderer picks V5s), each lapped 4 times in a row for 16 total ascents, with only a brief shake/rechalk between consecutive laps.**

  > "pick 4 boulder problems 2-4 grades below max (e.g. a V8 boulderer picks V5s). Lap problem #1 four times in a row with only a brief shake/rechalk between goes... After 4 laps, rest 5-10 min, then repeat on 3 more problems (4 problems x 4 laps = 16 ascents)."

- **Within the 4x4, rest is roughly equal to climbing time (~1:1 work:rest ratio) between laps, and 5-10 minutes of rest is taken between each of the four problems.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/training/too-weak-for-your-sport-project-heres-what-to-do-about-that/)_
  > "rest roughly equal to climbing time (~1:1 work:rest). After 4 laps, rest 5-10 min, then repeat on 3 more problems"

- **Two dedicated power-endurance / anaerobic-capacity sessions should be performed per week.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/training/too-weak-for-your-sport-project-heres-what-to-do-about-that/)_
  > "Two power-endurance sessions per week."

- **The article prescribes a 30/30 interval protocol: 30 seconds of all-out max-intensity climbing (RPE 9-10) followed by 30 seconds of rest, repeated 6 times back-to-back as one set; rest 10-15 minutes, then repeat, with well-conditioned climbers doing up to 4 sets of 6 intervals.**  
  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [climbing.com](https://www.climbing.com/skills/training/too-weak-for-your-sport-project-heres-what-to-do-about-that/)_
  > "30 seconds of all-out, max-intensity climbing (RPE 9-10) followed by 30 seconds of rest, repeated 6 times back-to-back. After completing 6 intervals, climbers take 10-15 minutes of rest before doing a second set of 6 intervals. Well-conditioned climbers can complete up to 4 sets of 6 intervals."

### Hangboard protocol deep dive  (7)

- **Max hangs protocol: hang a fixed edge for 7-10 seconds with added weight to near-failure, rest 3-5 minutes, for 3-5 sets, training the nervous system to recruit more motor units (~10s work per set).**

- **Repeaters protocol uses 7 seconds on / 3 seconds off for 6 reps per set, 3-5 sets, at a lower load than max hangs, producing ~40s+ time under tension per set to build strength-endurance.**  
  _search · vote 3-0 · [thehangboard.com](https://thehangboard.com/blogs/news/max-hangs-vs-repeaters)_
  > "Repeaters: classic 7s on / 3s off x6 reps per set, 3-5 sets, lower load but ~40s+ TUT per set, builds strength-endurance."

  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [thehangboard.com](https://thehangboard.com/blogs/news/max-hangs-vs-repeaters)_
  > "Repeater load ~60-80% MVC (beginners 40-50%)."

  _reasoning · vote 3-0 · [thehangboard.com](https://thehangboard.com/blogs/news/max-hangs-vs-repeaters)_
