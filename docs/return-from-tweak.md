# Return-From-Finger-Tweak Guide (KG-A7 mitigation)

A one-page guide for the **"finger tweaked but not injured"** episode — a niggle or strain you feel during or after a session that is *not* a diagnosed pulley tear. This is the most common intermediate finger scenario and, until now, had no protocol in this app. Closes [KG-A7](knowledge-gaps-archive.md#kg-a7--injury-prevention-dosing-is-partial-p2-g3)'s return-from-tweak half (the dosing half closed separately in #40). Evidence: [`research/return-from-tweak-evidence.md`](research/return-from-tweak-evidence.md).

This is deliberately a **printed reference, not app logic** — every numeric constant below is practitioner convention or a cross-tissue extrapolation, not a validated finger-specific rule, and no RCT ranks one return-from-tweak protocol over another. Nothing in this repo enforces, checks, or gates on any of it.

**Not medical advice.** This is a training de-escalation checklist, not a diagnosis. If a red flag in §4 applies, or a tweak isn't trending better on the timeline below, stop and see a hand/sports-medicine professional.

**Evidence labels used below** (see the linked evidence sweep for full sourcing):
- **VALIDATED** — primary-sourced / RCT / validated measurement.
- **CONVENTION** — practitioner consensus (coach/PT blog-level), not primary-validated.
- **EXTRAPOLATION** — a validated finding borrowed from a different tissue or population.

## 1. The pain gate — decide whether to load today

Adapted from the Silbernagel Achilles-tendinopathy loading model (**VALIDATED as an RCT — but EXTRAPOLATION here**: it's tendon, not the finger's annular-ligament pulleys, and was never tested in climbers). Rate pain 0–10 (NPRS) and check all four:

- [ ] Pain **during** loading stays ≤ 5/10
- [ ] Pain **after** loading stays ≤ 5/10
- [ ] Pain **the next morning** does not exceed 5/10
- [ ] Pain/stiffness is **not increasing week to week**

All four clear → continue loading at the current de-escalation stage (§2). Any one fails → drop back a stage or take a full rest day on that hand shape.

A tighter **CONVENTION** tiering some coaches use instead: ≤3/10 green (proceed), 3–5/10 amber (hold, don't progress), >5/10 red (back off) — the exact cutoff is unsettled even among practitioners; pick one tiering and stay consistent with it through the episode rather than switching mid-course.

## 2. De-escalation ladder — cut grip before you cut anything else

The mechanism is **VALIDATED**: an open-hand grip keeps flexor-tendon force vectors close to parallel with the bone, so the A2 pulley carries very little load; a full crimp (PIP flexed ≥ 90°) turns the A2 into a fulcrum and spikes pulley load. The staged ladder built on top of that mechanism is **CONVENTION**, cross-source but never trial-tested:

- [ ] **Stage 1 — open-hand only, big holds.** Isometric hangs/pulls on a large edge or jug, open-hand grip, ~20–40% effort (RPE 5–7), 3–5 reps × 30–45s. No half-crimp or full-crimp yet.
- [ ] **Stage 2 — controlled eccentrics, still open-hand.** Once Stage 1 clears the pain gate pain-free (or ≤3/10), add slow eccentric lowering, still open-hand only, still minimal pain.
- [ ] **Stage 3 — reintroduce grip position, easiest first.** Progress the *grip*, not just the load: open-hand → half-crimp → full crimp **last**, and only reintroduce full crimp at zero pain, zero swelling.

No source quantifies how much to cut *volume* for a tweak (this is genuinely silent in the evidence) — the deload playbook's "cut volume, hold intensity" doesn't transfer here. For a tweak, treat grip position and intensity as the levers you cut first, and reduce volume by feel rather than by a specific number.

## 3. Reload ramp — climbing back up

- [ ] Increase load roughly **2–5% per week** once the current stage is pain-free (**CONVENTION**, no validated finger-specific number).
- [ ] Advance the grip sequence in the same order as de-escalation: open-hand → half-crimp → full-crimp last.
- [ ] Low-intensity, high-frequency reloading is not a strength trade-off — a training-adaptation RCT in healthy climbers found low-intensity hangs built comparable finger strength to max-intensity hangs (**VALIDATED in healthy climbers; EXTRAPOLATION to tweak-return**, since it wasn't a return-from-injury study). This just removes one reason to rush back to heavy loading — it doesn't set a reload threshold by itself.
- [ ] There is no validated "safe to hang again" number — go by the pain gate (§1) clearing at each stage, not a calendar countdown.

## 4. Red flags — this isn't a tweak, stop and get evaluated

Cross-source **CONVENTION** (clinical-exam consensus). Any of the following means this is likely no longer a tweak — stop loading that hand shape and see a professional rather than continuing the ladder above:

- [ ] An audible **pop or snap** at the moment of onset
- [ ] **Swelling** beyond mild (moderate-to-immediate swelling suggests a tear, not a strain)
- [ ] **Bruising**
- [ ] Visible **bowstringing** (the flexor tendon lifting off the bone under load) — the single strongest red flag, and a **VALIDATED** clinical sign of a diagnosed pulley injury
- [ ] Pain that **limits range of motion**, pain **at rest**, or a noticeable **loss of force**
- [ ] **Sharp pain persisting beyond 1–2 weeks**, recurring swelling, nerve symptoms, or mechanical locking/catching

This is exactly where the tweak zone hands off to the diagnosed-injury literature (Schöffl grading, imaging, staged rehab timelines of 6+ weeks) — that territory is out of this guide's scope by design.
